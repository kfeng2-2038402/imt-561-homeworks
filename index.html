Documenting Iterative Process

Context of use: This clock is designed for quiet study or work spaces, such as a bedroom, study room, café corner, or personal workspace. I often find that when I study or work for a long time, especially when the outside weather feels gloomy, time can feel slow and it becomes harder to stay focused. Instead of asking users to constantly check a digital timer, this clock turns a focus session into a changing window scene. The goal is to make time feel more visual, ambient, and connected to the gradual shift from mental fog to clearer focus.

Final Clock 1: Focus Window Clock

Sketch iteration documentation

Design process: My first version was closer to a traditional clock. I used a sunset window scene and imagined the sun as the main time indicator. The sun moving from left to right was intended to represent minutes, while the sun moving from top to bottom was intended to represent hours. However, after sketching this idea, I realized that the mapping was not very easy to read. It did not clearly indicate a specific time as a clock, and users might not immediately understand how to interpret the sun’s position.

More importantly, the early version did not connect strongly enough to my actual context of use. My goal was not only to make a visually interesting clock, but to design something that could support the feeling of studying or working when I am tired, distracted, or mentally foggy. Because of this, I shifted the design from a literal sunset clock into an ambient focus timer. In the final version, the window does not represent the real time of day. Instead, it represents the progress of a focus session.

In the revised version, the scene starts as pale, gloomy, and foggy. As the session progresses, the fog clouds move outward from the center, the middle of the window becomes brighter, and the sky gradually shifts into warmer colors. This creates a “clearing the fog” effect, which connects to the feeling of slowly entering a better focus state. I also removed the hard trapezoid color layers from the earlier version and replaced them with a continuous gradient and soft atmospheric glow, so the window feels more calm and environmental rather than visually fragmented.

Time mapping: The sun’s movement no longer represents exact clock time. Instead, it represents the progress of the focus session. The fogginess at the beginning suggests the difficulty of starting work, while the clearing clouds and brighter center area show progress and growing focus. The warmer sky near the end gives a subtle sense of completion and accomplishment. This makes the clock less about reading the exact time and more about sensing time passing in an ambient way.

I applied several information visualization principles in the final version. For mapping, the sun movement, fog clearing, and color shift all correspond to the progress of the focus session. For motion, the fog clouds slowly move from the center toward both sides, making the “clearing” process visible. For color, the sky transitions from cool gray-blue tones to warmer yellow and orange tones, suggesting a shift from low energy to focus and completion. For hierarchy, the window frame, moving sun, and clearing fog guide the viewer’s attention without requiring them to constantly read numbers.

Self-reflection / future work: This version feels stronger than the early sunset clock because it is tied to a more specific use context. It does not just show that time is passing; it visualizes the experience of moving from distraction to clarity during a focus session. In a future version, I would add an optional session length control, such as 25 minutes for a Pomodoro session or 2 minutes for a quick demo. I would also consider adding a subtle completion state, such as a fully clear window or a short message, to help users feel a stronger sense of accomplishment.

This section documents the design iteration for my second final clock: the Boba Focus Clock.

Context of use: This clock is designed for studying or working in everyday spaces, such as a desk, bedroom, café corner, or study area. I often find that when I study or work for a long time, time feels slow and I can become tired or distracted. Instead of making users constantly check a digital timer, this clock embeds time into a familiar daily object: a cup of boba tea. It gives users a playful and low-pressure way to notice time passing during a focus session.

Final Clock 2: Boba Focus Clock

Sketch iteration documentation (paper/hand-drawn photos)

Design process: At first, I approached this design as a boba tea clock, using the folded angle of the straw to represent the hour and minute hands. This version kept the logic of a traditional clock while embedding time into a familiar everyday object: a cup of milk tea. However, as I continued to think about the context of use, I realized that turning it into “another kind of clock” did not create a very strong motivation for people to use it. In contrast, reframing it as a focus timer felt more meaningful and more desirable in the context of studying or working.

Because of this, I shifted the design into a focus timer for everyday work and study settings. In the final version, the liquid level represents the remaining duration of the focus session, the number of boba pearls represents the remaining minutes, and the straw no longer functions as a traditional clock hand. Instead, it becomes a subtle feedback mechanism that gently stirs the drink once every minute. This makes the passing of time visible without requiring the user to constantly read a digital number.

To make the design feel more immersive and better connected to the boba tea metaphor, I added several animation details. The boba pearls and ice cubes gently float even when the timer is not stirring, so the drink feels alive rather than static. When each minute passes, the straw slowly stirs the drink, causing the boba and ice cubes to shift slightly. The ice cubes also become smaller and gradually melt as the liquid level goes down. These changes make the design feel less like a decorative clock and more like an ambient, playful way to experience the passing of time.

I applied several information visualization principles in the final version. For hierarchy, the cup and liquid level are the main visual focus because they communicate the overall progress of the focus session. For mapping, the liquid height represents total remaining time, while the number of boba pearls represents remaining minutes. For motion, the gentle floating animation keeps the clock feeling alive without becoming too distracting, and the slower stirring animation marks each minute in a subtle way. For color, I used warm milk tea tones with pale blue ice cubes so the design feels playful, readable, and connected to the drink metaphor.

Self-reflection / future work: This version is more meaningful than my early sketch because it connects the clock to a specific use context: staying focused while studying or working. The design does not try to replace a precise digital timer, but it creates a softer way to sense time passing. In a future version, I would add an optional mode switch between a 25-minute Pomodoro session and a shorter testing/demo session. I would also consider adding a clearer completion state, such as an empty cup or a gentle message, so users can clearly feel that the focus session is finished.

by Yongxi Chen — source code

Context of use: This sketch represents the rhythm of box breathing, a meditation technique used to calm the mind and reduce stress by maintaining a steady 4-4-4-4 breathing cycle: breathe in, hold, breathe out, hold. It is designed for mindfulness practice, stress management sessions, and focus exercises where users need visual and temporal guidance to regulate their breathing.

Ex 11

Design process:

  1. Square path visualization: The breathing cycle is represented by a square with four edges, where each edge corresponds to one breathing phase. A colored line progressively draws around the square, providing clear visual tracking of the 4-second intervals. The square shape reinforces the "box breathing" concept.
  2. Color-coded phases: Each of the four phases uses a distinct teal-to-gray gradient color (teal for inhale, light blue for first hold, mint for exhale, gray for second hold).
  3. Countdown timer with neon glow: Large text displays the current phase label. The neon glow effect on the lines creates a calming, modern aesthetic while maintaining high visibility against the starry background.

Self-reflection / future work: Add customizable breathing durations (e.g., 3-3-3-3 or 5-5-5-5 cycles) to accommodate different skill levels. Include audio cues or gentle sound at phase transitions to support eyes-closed practice.

by Teresa Wang — source code

Context: This clock is designed for people who love making pancakes. It works as a fun, visual timer to use while cooking, helping users track how long their pancakes have been frying through animation and color changes.

Ex 12

Design process: For my pancake clock, I designed the layout as a pancake cooking simulator. It shows a pan frying pancakes and then dropping them onto a plate, which makes the whole clock feel like a small kitchen scene. The animation was an intentional choice because it imitates a real cooking environment and makes the passage of time more visual and engaging.

The color change of the pancakes is another key design choice. They start light and gradually turn golden brown as time passes. This helps people understand how long the pancake has been cooking without needing to read numbers, giving a more natural sense of time. I also added a countdown counter below the plate to give users a clear, easy-to-read time reference, balancing fun visuals with functional feedback.

Since I wasn’t able to hand-draw detailed pancakes due to limited time, the visuals could have looked more polished if I had more time to refine them. I also chose warm, high-contrast colors (browns and yellows) for better visibility and accessibility.

Self-reflection / future work: In the future, I would like to add more realistic pancake textures and steam effects to make the cooking scene feel livelier. I also plan to add features like letting users flip the pancakes with a click to include simple interactivity.
